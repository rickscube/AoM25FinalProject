ANTIQUITY - 1831

The Balanced Fluid

The Belief: Health relies on the balance of four fluids: blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile.

Reality: Pathology is anchored in organs, not fluid imbalance.

Click to Inspect Case [+]

Archival Note: The Coal Miner's Leeches

Originating with Galen (129-216 AD), this theory dominated medicine for 2,000 years. Physicians believed illness was merely fluids "out of balance".

The Persistence of Error: Even in 1831, after the discovery of anatomical pathology, Dr. James Craufurd Gregory treated a coal miner's lung disease with leeches. Why? Because while he could diagnose the organ damage, he had no tools to treat it, so he fell back on the obsolete "humoral" cure to provide "some relief".

1630 CE

The Simulated Plague

The Belief: The plague in Milan was a "simulated" hoax caused by "Anointers" smearing poison on walls.

Reality: Yersinia pestis (Bubonic Plague).

Click to Inspect Case [+]

Archival Note: The Yellow Filth Conspiracy

During the 1630 outbreak in Milan, citizens refused to believe in the disease. When yellow streaks appeared on doors and benches, mass hysteria erupted, blaming "Untori" (anointers) for spreading poison as part of a foreign plot.

The Cost of Denial: To force the public to accept reality, the Board of Health eventually ordered the naked corpses of an entire family to be carted through the crowded streets during the festival of Pentecost.

1770 - 1791

The Fabricated Angle

The Belief: The "Facial Angle" proves Europeans are biologically superior to other races.

Reality: Scientific Racism / Manipulated Data.

Click to Inspect Case [+]

Archival Note: The Missing Monkey

Dutch anatomist Petrus Camper claimed that a steeper "facial angle" indicated beauty and intelligence, placing Europeans at the top and Africans closer to apes.

The Deception: Camper’s original sketches included a "death's head monkey" (squirrel monkey) which had a facial angle of 50 degrees—too close to humans for his theory to work. In his final publication, he swapped it for an Orangutan to make the racist hierarchy appear scientifically "smooth".

1854 CE

The Broad Street Pump

The Belief: Cholera is spread by "Miasma" (bad airs) and vapors.

Reality: Waterborne Bacteria (Cholera).

Click to Inspect Case [+]

Archival Note: The Map as Evidence

Victorian London believed "bad air" caused disease. Physician John Snow defied this by mapping cholera cases in the West End, finding they clustered around a single water pump on Broad Street.

The Intervention: Snow didn't just treat patients; he removed the handle of the pump. This moment marked the shift from vague environmental theories to modern epidemiology.

1900 - 1950s

The Catch-All Diagnosis

The Belief: "Hysteria" (from the Greek for uterus) causes female emotional volatility.

Reality: Systemic sexism in medical diagnostics.

Click to Inspect Case [+]

Archival Note: From Massage to Machine

For centuries, women's medical complaints were dismissed as "Hysteria." Galen originally prescribed marriage or pelvic massage by a midwife to "calm" the womb. By the late 19th century, doctors sought efficiency.

The Invention: Dr. J. Mortimer Granville invented the electromechanical vibrator not for pleasure, but as a medical tool to induce "hysterical paroxysm" (orgasm) to relieve symptoms more quickly in a clinical setting.
